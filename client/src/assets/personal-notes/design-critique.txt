UNDERGROUND — DESIGN CRITIQUE
February 24, 2026
Critique by Interface Craft methodology (Josh Puckett)
Focus: Visual design, typography system, storytelling coherence
Scope: Ignoring animation polish (known WIP), evaluating interactions as storytelling devices

================================================================================
CONTEXT
================================================================================

Underground is a daily content discovery app for mobile — a curated feed of news,
facts, philosophy, games, history, trivia, and long-form storytelling, framed as
a counterpoint to mainstream media. The target user is someone on a break —
subway, lunch, couch — looking for something thoughtful and surprising.

The emotional arc the app intends: enter through glowing brackets, discover
something underground, leave with the meditative message "look up, breathe,
notice the person across from you."

================================================================================
FIRST IMPRESSIONS
================================================================================

The app has a clear vision and real ambition. The color palette is warm, the
card-based structure gives each module its own identity, and the content curation
is genuinely interesting — The Amber Room, the thought experiment about unseen
objects, sharks being older than trees. The bottle cap flip card is charming.
The "Seen It All?" closing text is the kind of thing that makes an app feel human.

But when I scroll through the feed as a whole, it reads like a color sample book.
Ten distinct background colors, nine content modules, three different interaction
models, all at the same visual volume. The individual cards are each doing
interesting things. The composition of those cards into a sequence doesn't tell
the story the app wants to tell.

================================================================================
VISUAL DESIGN
================================================================================

ISSUE: Ten competing background colors with no tonal logic
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The feed uses warm yellow (#f4ca5b), bright blue (#2C63C7), muted tan (#b9acaa),
sage green (#c1cea9), dark forest green (#597c3b), soft pink (#F6AFE9), deep
magenta (#9B3A66), burnt orange (#EB9131), rust brown (#a96424), and purple
(#967bd2). Some are dusty and muted, some are vibrant and saturated, some are
dark. There's no shared tonal family or progression. Scrolling through the feed,
the eye bounces between unrelated hues rather than moving through a coherent
atmosphere.

  QUICK FIX: Don't overhaul the palette entirely — instead, unify the saturation
  and brightness range. Pull the vibrant outliers (pink, blue, magenta) toward the
  same muted warmth as the ones that already work (tan, sage, rust). Desaturate
  the pink by ~15%, warm the blue, and soften the magenta. Keep the hues distinct
  but make them feel like they were mixed from the same paint set. This is a
  color.ts tweak, not a redesign.


ISSUE: Label contrast is inverted from its importance
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Every module uses a lighter tint of its background for the category label (e.g.,
#feeaae on #f4ca5b for Above Ground, #dbd3d2 on #b9acaa for Thought Experiment).
This makes the labels the lowest-contrast element on each card. But labels are
the primary wayfinding mechanism — they tell the user what kind of content they're
looking at before they read anything. The most important orientation element is
the hardest to read.

  QUICK FIX: Flip the label strategy. Instead of lighter-than-background, go
  darker-than-background by 2-3 steps. On the yellow card, use a deep amber
  instead of pale cream. On the tan card, use a warm brown. This keeps the
  subtle aesthetic but makes labels actually readable. Update the LABEL values
  in colors.ts — no component changes needed.


ISSUE: Flat typographic hierarchy within cards
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The type scale defines 9 variants, but the working range is narrow. Title is
20px bold, subhead is 15px bold, detail is 15px bold, body is 15px light. The
distinction between subhead, detail, and body is weight alone at the same size.
That's not a hierarchy users can perceive at scanning speed. And the title-to-body
ratio (20px to 15px) is only 1.33x — not enough visual separation.

  THIS NEEDS A RETHINK — see the Typography System Proposal section below.


ISSUE: Every collapsed card speaks at the same volume
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
All collapsed cards render their preview content in type-title (20px Satoshi-Bold).
The trivia question, the thought experiment snippet, the micro-history title, the
wiki teaser, the riddle — all identical. Nothing is louder or quieter. The feed
becomes a wall of bold 20px text blocks in different-colored boxes.

  QUICK FIX: This resolves naturally with the typography system proposal below.
  Static cards (content IS the card) stay in headline type. Expandable cards use
  a lighter teaser style when collapsed to signal "there's more here." Two voices,
  one rule.


ISSUE: Quarter cards are overloaded for their footprint
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
At 195x190px, the Riddle card packs a 24-word question. The Word of the Day card
fits a word, pronunciation, part of speech, and definition. These small cards hold
paragraph-scale content in thumbnail-scale containers.

  QUICK FIX: For quarter cards, truncate aggressively. Riddle collapsed could show
  just "I speak without a mouth..." (first clause only). Word of the Day could
  drop the definition when collapsed and show only the word + pronunciation. Let
  the tap-to-reveal interaction carry the rest. Less text in a small space reads
  as confident, not empty.

================================================================================
INTERFACE DESIGN
================================================================================

ISSUE: No focusing mechanism — the feed is a flat list
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
When the app loads, the user sees the date header, then Above Ground, then Factle
+ Thought Experiment side-by-side. Nothing says "start here." The date header is
visually dominant but functionally decorative. Everything competes equally.

  QUICK FIX: Make Above Ground taller than the other cards when collapsed — even
  30-40px more gives it visual primacy without changing the layout grid. It's the
  "front page" of the daily edition. Let it feel like one.


ISSUE: Content order doesn't build a narrative arc
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Games — the most stimulating module — sits in the middle. The user goes from a
philosophical thought experiment to skiing down a mountain, then back to trivia,
then to a meditative plea to breathe. The tonal arc breaks and never recovers.

  QUICK FIX: Move Games up (right after Above Ground + Factle/Thought Experiment).
  Front-load the energy. Then let the feed descend: Trivia/Riddle > Micro History >
  On This Day / Word of the Day > Wiki Summary > Seen It All. The sequence becomes:
  stimulate > play > quiz > learn > reflect > rest. This is a Mockup.tsx JSX
  reorder — no component changes.


ISSUE: The emotional climax is presented as a footnote
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
"Seen It All?" is the thesis statement of the entire app. But it's plain white
type-prose text with standard padding — indistinguishable from wiki body text.

  QUICK FIX: Wrap it in a card with a distinct background (or a very subtle dark
  card, slightly lighter than #171719 — like #1e1e22). Give it more top padding
  (3-4x card-padding). Use a slightly larger type size or the title weight. One
  card wrapper + spacing change. The content is already perfect.


ISSUE: Expanded states add text, not dimension
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
When a card expands, the user gets more paragraphs in the same type style on the
same background color. The visual transformation is minimal — the card gets taller.

  QUICK FIX: For the expandable cards (Above Ground, Micro History, Wiki Summary),
  the expanded body text should feel distinctly different from the title. Using
  Satoshi-Light (body weight) at a comfortable reading size with more generous
  line-height already exists in the system — just make sure there's a real visual
  gap between the headline and the body. A horizontal rule, a bit of extra spacing,
  or a shift to the prose type class for expanded long-form would help. Small
  layout adjustments per card, not new components.


ISSUE: Factle bottle cap has no interaction hint
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
When collapsed, the Factle card shows a label and a bottle cap image with no
prompt. A first-time user doesn't know it flips.

  QUICK FIX: Add a subtle "tap to flip" teaser in type-body below the cap, or
  use the same low-contrast approach the labels use. Even a small text cue makes
  the interaction discoverable without cluttering the card.

================================================================================
CONSISTENCY & CONVENTIONS
================================================================================

ISSUE: Three different patterns for card headers
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Expandable cards use the CardHeader component (label + button). Static cards use
a manual label div. Word of the Day uses a tiny PNG image. Three treatments for
the same UI element.

  QUICK FIX: Use CardHeader everywhere. For non-expandable cards, pass
  showButton={false} (the prop already exists). Replace the Word of the Day PNG
  with a text label "WORD OF THE DAY". One component, one pattern.


ISSUE: Interactive cards give no affordance signal
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Expandable cards show a chevron/X. But the Factle flip card and Reveal cards
(Trivia, Riddle) are also interactive yet show no visual indicator. Non-expandable
static cards (On This Day) look identical to reveal cards but do nothing on tap.

  QUICK FIX: For reveal cards, add a subtle indicator — a small "tap" icon or a
  different button icon (like a "?" or a refresh arrow) in the CardHeader button
  slot. This distinguishes "tap to reveal" from "static content" without adding
  visual noise. Could also just use a different icon in the existing button spot.


ISSUE: Text color doesn't follow a consistent rule
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
On Micro History (sage green), title is white and body is black — two colors on
one card. Trivia (dark magenta) uses black text. Wiki (similar-value dark green)
uses white. On This Day (dark rust) uses black. No predictable relationship
between background darkness and text color.

  QUICK FIX: Establish a simple rule and stick to it. For example: backgrounds
  above 50% lightness get black text, below 50% get white text. Apply
  universally. For Micro History, pick one text color (white works better on sage
  green). Update the TEXT values in colors.ts. This is a 15-minute pass through
  the color file.


ISSUE: Uniform 190px collapsed height across all card types
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Every collapsed card — full-width, half-width, quarter — is 190px. This creates
a metronomic rhythm where every row feels identical.

  QUICK FIX: Give full-width expandable cards a slightly taller collapsed height
  (220-230px). Keep quarter cards at 190px. This creates two rhythms — "big
  cards" and "small cards" — which is enough variation to break the monotony.
  Update collapsedHeight constants per card.

================================================================================
USER CONTEXT
================================================================================

The person opening Underground is taking a pause. They're curious, not urgent.
They want to feel smarter, more connected, or gently entertained.

Right now, the app asks the user to do a lot of cognitive switching. Each card has
a different color, a different interaction model (expand? flip? reveal? static?),
and a different content type. The experience is closer to walking through a craft
market with twelve stalls than reading a beautifully paced magazine.

The intro animation (glowing corner brackets: blue > white > green) creates a
moment of ceremony. It says "something is about to be revealed." But what follows
is an immediate bombardment of color and content, and the meditative close most
users will never scroll to.

What "uncommon care" looks like: pacing. The app knows its story — it has a
ceremonial open and a contemplative close. What it's missing is the middle that
carries the arc. A sense of descent from stimulation to stillness.

TYPOGRAPHY SYSTEM — IMPLEMENTED

================================================================================
================================================================================

STATUS: LIVE — applied February 24, 2026

GOAL: Fewer types. Bigger and bolder. A clear system that distinguishes between
"this content IS the card" (static) vs "this content PREVIEWS the card" (expandable).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
THE PROBLEM WITH THE OLD SYSTEM
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The old system had 9 type variants:
  display, label, title, subhead, detail, body, teaser, prose, mono

In practice, 3 of these were nearly identical:
  - subhead: 15px Satoshi-Bold
  - detail:  15px Satoshi-Bold
  - body:    15px Satoshi-Light
  (same size, only weight differed)

And 2 were only used once:
  - display: date header only
  - mono: footer only

This left title (20px bold) doing ALL the heavy lifting — headlines, teasers,
questions, answers, truncated text all looked the same. Every collapsed card
spoke at the same volume.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
CURRENT SYSTEM: 5 TYPES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Cut from 9 to 5. Each has one clear job.

  TYPE        FONT              SIZE              WEIGHT  LEADING   JOB
  --------    ----------------  ----------------  ------  -------   --------------------------
  display     Satoshi-Bold      dynamic           700     —         Date header. One instance.
  label       Sora              10px × scale      700     —         Category tags. Uppercase.
  headline    Satoshi-Bold      22px × scale      700     1.2       Card titles + static content.
  reading     Satoshi-Regular   15px × scale      400     1.5       Expanded body text.
  caption     Satoshi-Regular   13px × scale      400     1.38      Teasers, metadata, footer.

Five types. Three fonts (Satoshi-Bold, Satoshi-Regular, Sora).

Key size ratios:
  headline / reading = 1.47x (up from 1.33x — noticeably clearer hierarchy)
  headline / caption = 1.69x (bold title vs light secondary — unmistakable)
  reading / caption  = 1.15x (body vs metadata — subtle but perceptible)

Design decisions:
  - Caption at 13px (not 12px as originally proposed) to prevent readability
    issues on smaller phones. At --scale 0.82 (iPhone SE), 13px → 10.7px
    actual — tight but readable. 12px would have hit 9.8px, below the floor.
  - Reading uses Satoshi-Regular (400) instead of Satoshi-Light (300).
    Light was too fragile on dark backgrounds (Wiki Summary on dark green).
  - Each role has its own line-height: headline at 1.2 (tight, scannable),
    reading at 1.5 (generous, comfortable), caption at 1.38 (in between).
    This creates rhythm shifts between roles that reinforce hierarchy.

What was merged and why:
  - subhead   → headline (distinguished by accent color, not size)
  - detail    → caption (pronunciation, metadata = small secondary info)
  - body      → reading (clearer name: "this is for reading paragraphs")
  - teaser    → caption (teasers and metadata serve the same structural role)
  - prose     → reading (one reading size — 15px scaled is comfortable on mobile)
  - mono      → caption with inline font-family override (one instance)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
CAPTION USAGE CONVENTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Caption handles two psychologically different jobs:

  1. TEASER (collapsed expandable cards) — an invitation: "tap me, there's more"
     → Use full opacity in the card's accent or secondary color
     → This is reader-facing content. It should feel like a whisper of what's inside.

  2. METADATA (source attribution, pronunciation, footer) — context
     → Use reduced opacity (60-70%) or a quieter color
     → This is incidental information. It should step back.

Same CSS class (.type-caption), different visual treatment through color/opacity.
Apple does this constantly — one type style, multiple voices through color weight.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HOW THE 5 TYPES MAP TO CARD STATES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

STATIC CARDS (content IS the card — Trivia, Riddle, On This Day, Factle):

  ┌─────────────────────────────┐
  │ label: "TRIVIA"             │
  │                             │
  │ headline: "What year did    │
  │ the first iPhone launch?"   │
  └─────────────────────────────┘

  The headline IS the content. Big, bold, fills the card.
  When a reveal card is tapped, the answer appears in headline too (centered).


WORD OF THE DAY (static, mixed roles):

  ┌─────────────────────────────┐
  │ label: "WORD OF THE DAY"    │
  │                             │
  │ headline: "Inure"           │
  │ caption (0.7): [IN-YOOR]   │  ← metadata, reduced opacity
  │ caption: to accustom to...  │  ← definition, full opacity
  └─────────────────────────────┘


EXPANDABLE CARDS — COLLAPSED (Above Ground, Micro History, Wiki Summary, Games):

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ label: "MICRO HISTORY"              [v] │
  │                                         │
  │ headline: "Why notebooks are            │
  │ usually lined"                          │
  │ caption: "Early mass-produced paper..." │  ← lighter teaser signals expandability
  └─────────────────────────────────────────┘

  The weight contrast between headline (22px Bold) and caption (13px Regular)
  is the visual cue that this card expands. Static cards don't have this lighter
  text, so the user learns: caption below headline = tap for more.


EXPANDABLE CARDS — EXPANDED:

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ label: "MICRO HISTORY"              [x] │
  │                                         │
  │ headline: "Why notebooks are            │
  │ usually lined"                          │
  │                                         │
  │ reading: "Early mass-produced paper     │
  │ varied in quality, and lines helped     │
  │ guide handwriting when ink bled easily. │
  │ The ruled lines became standard..."     │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Caption teaser replaced by reading text — full paragraphs in Satoshi-Regular
  at a comfortable reading size with generous line-height. The shift from
  caption to reading is the visual reward for expanding.


WIKI SUMMARY — EXPANDED (long-form, multi-section):

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ label: "WIKI SUMMARY"              [x] │
  │                                         │
  │ headline: "The Amber Room"              │
  │                                         │
  │ headline: "A Room Made of Sunlight"     │  ← accent color distinguishes from title
  │ reading: "In 1701, King Frederick I..." │
  │                                         │
  │ headline: "The Gift"                    │
  │ reading: "In 1716, Frederick William    │
  │ I gave the entire room..."              │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Section headings reuse headline in the accent color (#b5e3af on dark green).
  No separate subhead type needed — color does the job.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
THE RULE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

When building a new card or adding content, ask one question:

  "Is this the content, or is this about the content?"

  If it IS the content      → headline
  If it's ABOUT the content → reading (expanded) or caption (collapsed/metadata)
  If it's a category tag    → label

And for caption specifically:

  If it's inviting the user in → full opacity, accent color
  If it's just context         → reduced opacity (0.6–0.7)

Three content types. One rule. Done.
